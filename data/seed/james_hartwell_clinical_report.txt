[SECTION: History of Presenting Complaint]

Mr Hartwell first presented to your surgery in 2017 with a three-month history of
progressive fatigue, bilateral arthralgia predominantly affecting the small joints of
the hands, and a photosensitive malar rash. At that time, investigations confirmed
a strongly positive antinuclear antibody (ANA titre 1:1280, speckled pattern) and
elevated anti-double-stranded DNA antibodies (anti-dsDNA 187 IU/mL, reference
<10 IU/mL), with low complement (C3 0.52 g/L, C4 0.06 g/L), consistent with a
diagnosis of SLE. He was commenced on hydroxychloroquine 400mg daily and
maintained on low-dose prednisolone 5mg daily for approximately two years before
this was successfully weaned.

His disease course has been characterised by intermittent flares, predominantly
manifesting with mucocutaneous and musculoskeletal involvement. In 2020, he
developed symptoms consistent with secondary Sjögren's syndrome — severe
xerostomia, keratoconjunctivitis sicca, and parotid gland swelling — confirmed on
minor salivary gland biopsy (focal lymphocytic sialadenitis, focus score 2).

The October 2023 admission represented his most severe flare to date. He presented
to Dr Patel with three weeks of worsening fatigue, new-onset chest pain worsened
by lying flat, bilateral hand arthralgia, and significant urinary proteinuria on
dipstick (protein +++, blood ++). He was admitted urgently to Ward 7B under joint
Nephrology and Rheumatology care.

[SECTION: Inpatient Treatment]

Mr Hartwell received three pulses of intravenous methylprednisolone 500mg on
days 1–3 of admission (11–13 October 2023), followed by oral prednisolone
60mg daily. He was subsequently started on mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) 2g daily
(in two divided doses of 1g BD), which was titrated as tolerated. Given the
confirmed APS, therapeutic anticoagulation with warfarin was commenced with a
target INR of 2.5–3.5.

He was discharged on 19 October 2023 in an improved condition with:
- Prednisolone 60mg od (tapering schedule: reduce by 10mg every 2 weeks, guided by
  clinical response and renal function)
- Hydroxychloroquine 200mg BD (dose reduced pending ophthalmology review for
  cumulative toxicity screening — he has received >5 years of therapy)
- Mycophenolate mofetil 1g BD (to be increased to 1.5g BD at this outpatient visit
  if tolerated)
- Warfarin (current INR 2.8 — within target range)
- Omeprazole 20mg od (gastric protection)
- Calcium + Vitamin D supplement
- LMWH bridging (completed)

[SECTION: Management Plan and Recommendations]

1. LUPUS NEPHRITIS (Class III): Response to treatment is encouraging. Continue MMF
   — increasing dose to 1.5g BD as planned. Continue prednisolone tapering schedule.
   Repeat renal function and urine PCR in 4 weeks. Refer for consideration of
   belimumab (B-lymphocyte stimulator inhibitor) at next clinic if MMF response
   is maintained — may facilitate steroid sparing.

2. ANTIPHOSPHOLIPID SYNDROME: Continue warfarin, target INR 2.5–3.5. Patient
   counselled regarding the importance of anticoagulation given prior DVT risk and
   positive aPL on two occasions. Repeat aPL testing in 12 weeks.

3. AIHA: Haemoglobin improving on immunosuppression. Continue monitoring FBC.
   Haematology follow-up in 3 months.

4. PERICARDITIS/SEROSITIS: Clinically resolved. Echo to be repeated in 3 months
   to confirm resolution of effusion.

5. SJÖGREN'S SYMPTOMS: Ophthalmology referral made. Continue Hypromellose drops
   and saliva substitutes. Pilocarpine considered but deferred pending cardiac review.

6. HYDROXYCHLOROQUINE RETINOPATHY SCREENING: Ophthalmology appointment scheduled
